The system, in three charges

Low, flat tax — and contributions that stay small.

Belarus taxes personal income at a flat 13%, self-employment at 10%, and entrepreneurs and companies at 20%. Social contributions are nominally high but, for solo workers, sit on a low fixed floor — or are folded into the self-employed rate entirely.

10–20%

Income tax — low and mostly flat

Self-employment is taxed at 10%, standard personal income and dividends at 13%, and entrepreneurs and companies at 20%. A 25% band applies to personal income above 220,000 BYN (~€62k) a year — high enough that most contractors never reach it.

Contributions — but on a low floor

Social contributions (ФСЗН) are 35%, but a solo entrepreneur pays them on a minimum base of just 12× the monthly minimum wage — roughly €860 a year — not on full income. The self-employed rate has them baked in already.

VAT — usually not your problem

Standard VAT is 20%, but the self-employed regime sits outside VAT, and services exported to a foreign client are generally zero-rated or out of scope. It rarely touches a solo contractor billing abroad.

A year in the life of a Belarusian contractor's taxes

Day 1
Pick a status and register

Self-employment registers through the tax authority's app in minutes; an entrepreneur (IP) or company (OOO) registers with the tax office / registry. Status determines everything that follows.

Monthly (self-employed)
Declare income, pay 10% in the app

The self-employed app calculates 10% on declared receipts — contributions included — and you pay in-app. Income from a foreign client stays at 10%; the 20% band only touches income from Belarusian companies over 60,000 BYN.

Annually (entrepreneur)
File income tax, settle ФСЗН by 31 March

An IP files income tax on profit (income minus documented expenses) and pays the social-contribution floor for the year by 31 March of the following year.

Watch continuously
Currency rules and the payment rail

Foreign-currency proceeds face currency-control rules on the Belarus side — and on the payer's side, sanctions screening and a working bank route decide whether the money arrives at all.

⚠️
Before you pay

Sanctions and payment-rail restrictions may apply when paying into Belarus, and they differ by jurisdiction and change often. This sits outside the tax picture below — screen your counterparty and check compliance in your own jurisdiction before committing.

Same rate, different net

Cases that change the final number

In Belarus the status and the payment route matter more than the headline rate. These move the outcome most.

🎯

Which status they register

Self-employed (10% all-in), entrepreneur (20% + a small floor) and company (20% then 13% on dividends) don't converge — the burden can run from 10% to nearly 40% at the same income. It's the biggest lever on this page.

Big swing
🌍

Billing a foreign client

The self-employed 20% band only applies to income from Belarusian companies above 60,000 BYN. Income from a foreign client stays at the flat 10% — which is exactly the cross-border contractor case.

Stays 10%
🧾

No expense deduction if self-employed

The 10% self-employed rate is charged on gross revenue, with no deduction for costs. For work with heavy expenses, an entrepreneur or company — taxed on profit — can come out ahead despite the higher headline rates.

Depends
💻

High-Tech Park (ПВТ) residency

The HTP long gave IT companies near-zero profit tax and reduced contributions. Preferences remain but have narrowed in recent years. Still worth checking if the contractor operates through an HTP company.

Can lower
📈

Crossing the high-income bands

Personal income over 220,000 BYN (~€62k) a year meets a 25% rate, and very high entrepreneur income can reach 30%. Comfortably above where a typical single-client contractor lands, but worth knowing at the top end.

💸

Dividend timing

Company owners once used 6% or 0% dividend rates for profit left undistributed for three or five years. Those reliefs are being phased out, so the 13% standard rate is the safe planning assumption now.
